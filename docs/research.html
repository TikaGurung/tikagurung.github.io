Ph.D. Dissertation Work

High-Resolution Climate Simulations and Impacts of Local Human Factors on Hydroclimate and Glacier Dynamics in High Mountain Asia

My dissertation examines how large-scale irrigation across South Asia perturbs the regional hydroclimate system and remote glacier dynamics in the Himalaya and Karakoram, using high-resolution WRF regional climate models.

University of Nebraska-Lincoln · Expected Summer 2026

🔬 Ongoing Work: Two active projects investigate how irrigation-driven climate changes propagate into moisture transport across South Asia and glacier mass balance in High Mountain Asia. Figures and preliminary results are highlighted on the Home page →

Evaluating the Performance of High-Resolution Climate Simulations over the Central Himalaya and Karakoram
Gurung, T.R., Chen, L., Ali, S.H., Rowe, C., Arriola, F.M. and Ritzema, R.S.
Climate Dynamics, 64(3), p.120 · 2026

This study benchmarks WRF simulations against observations across the complex terrain of the Central Himalaya and Karakoram, evaluating their skill in reproducing temperature, precipitation, and atmospheric circulation — a critical step before using these models for impact studies.

DOI: 10.1007/s00382-025-08030-x →
Understanding the Influence of Soil Moisture on Heatwave Characteristics in the Contiguous United States
Gurung, T.R. and Chen, L.
Environmental Research Letters, 19(6) · 2024

Using observational data and model simulations, this paper quantifies how antecedent soil moisture deficits amplify heatwave intensity, duration, and spatial extent across the contiguous US — highlighting land-atmosphere feedbacks as a key driver of extreme heat events.

DOI: 10.1088/1748-9326/ad4dbb →
